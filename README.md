DIABETIC RETINOPATHY AI-POWERED HEALTH CARE CONSULTOR/DETECTOR --> 

Retinopathy Detector is an AI-powered retinal screening platform designed to detect early signs of diabetic retinopathy before vision loss occurs. Instead of relying solely on specialists to manually examine retinal scans, the system uses artificial intelligence and deep learning models to analyze fundus images and identify micro-level retinal abnormalities within seconds.

The platform focuses on detecting Non-Proliferative Diabetic Retinopathy (NPDR) — the stage where intervention can prevent permanent blindness.

By combining computer vision, cloud processing, and automated screening, Retinopathy Detector
transforms retinal analysis from a specialist-only task into an accessible diagnostic tool that can beused in clinics, diabetes centers, and community health programs.


How It Works:

• Image Capture – A technician captures a retinal fundus image using a handheld retinal
camera or smartphone-linked fundus device.

• Image Upload – The retinal image is uploaded to the Retinopathy Detector cloud platform.

• AI Analysis – Deep learning models such as CNNs and transformer-based vision models
analyze the image to detect retinal anomalies including microaneurysms, hemorrhages, and
hard exudates.

• Lesion Mapping – The system performs semantic segmentation, marking and mapping every
detected lesion to create a visual damage map of the retina.

• Risk Classification – The AI classifier determines the severity level of diabetic retinopathysuch as No DR, Mild NPDR, Moderate NPDR, or Severe NPDR.

• Automated Diagnostic Report – Within seconds the system generates a structured report
containing detected abnormalities, risk level classification, AI confidence score, and referral recommendation if needed.

• Early Referral and Monitoring – Patients with high-risk results are flagged for specialist
consultation while others can be monitored through periodic scans.

Retinopathy Detector enables early screening at scale, allowing healthcare workers, diabetes
clinics, and primary care centers to identify retinal damage long before symptoms appear. 

The goal is simple: detect early, intervene early, and prevent blindness
